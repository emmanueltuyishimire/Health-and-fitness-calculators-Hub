RMR Calculator

Calculate your Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR)—the number of calories your body burns while at rest. Understanding your RMR is the foundational step in managing your weight and is used to calculate your TDEE.

How to Use the RMR Calculator

Our RMR Calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, widely considered the most accurate method for estimating resting metabolic rate. Follow these simple steps to find your baseline calorie burn.

  1. Select Your Gender: The formula adjusts for physiological differences between males and females.
  2. Choose Your Units: Select "Metric" (kg, cm) or "Imperial" (lbs, inches).
  3. Enter Your Age: Your metabolic rate changes over your lifespan.
  4. Enter Your Height and Weight: These are key variables in determining your body mass.
  5. Calculate RMR: Click the button to see the number of calories your body would burn in a day at rest.
  6. Your Next Step: Your RMR is just the beginning. Take your RMR result and plug it into the TDEE Calculator to find your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE).

Understanding Resting Metabolic Rate

What is RMR?

Your Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR) is the amount of energy your body expends while at rest in a comfortable, neutral environment. This energy is used for vital functions like breathing, circulating blood, and controlling body temperature. It is the largest component of your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE), typically accounting for 60-75% of the calories you burn each day.

RMR is very similar to BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate), but is measured under less strict conditions. For most practical purposes, the two are interchangeable and provide a foundational understanding of your metabolism.

Factors Influencing RMR

Your RMR is determined by several factors, including:

  • Body Composition: Lean muscle mass burns more calories at rest than fat mass. The more muscle you have, the higher your RMR.
  • Age: RMR tends to decrease with age, largely due to a natural loss of muscle mass.
  • Gender: Men typically have a higher RMR than women because they generally have more muscle mass.
  • Genetics: Some people are born with a naturally higher or lower metabolism.
Common Mistakes
  • Confusing RMR with TDEE: Using your RMR as your daily calorie target is a major error. RMR doesn't include activity. Always use your RMR to calculate your TDEE in our TDEE Calculator.
  • Not Updating Your RMR: As you lose or gain weight, your RMR will change. You should recalculate it after every 5-10 lbs (or 2-5 kg) of weight change to keep your calorie targets accurate.
  • Using It as a Rigid Rule: RMR is an estimate. Use it as a starting point, monitor your weight and body composition over 2-4 weeks, and adjust your calorie intake up or down as needed.
Pro Tips & Quick Hacks
  • Prioritize Protein: When in a calorie deficit, a higher protein intake helps preserve muscle mass, which in turn helps keep your RMR from dropping as much. Use a Protein Intake Calculator to find your target.
  • Focus on Building Muscle: The best long-term strategy to boost your RMR is to increase your lean body mass through consistent resistance training.
  • Stay Hydrated: Proper hydration is necessary for all metabolic processes. Use the Water Intake Calculator to estimate your needs.
